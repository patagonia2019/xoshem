Q. What does mean Xoshem?

A. Xoshen means "wind" in Tehuelche language, and I took the name from a legend of this people. The Tehuelches were one of the original inhabitants of Patagonia.

Q. Which tells the legend about Xoshem?

A. The tehuelches told the original explorers that in the beginning of time, Kóoch (the creator god) lived in darkness and was overwhelmed by a great loneliness, so he cried for a long time. His tears created the sea (Arrok), and when he realized that the water soon cover everything, sighed, creating the wind (Xoshem). This wind divided the waters and created the mainland and the islands. To achieve to see his creation cleaned the darkness of the sky and created Xáleshen (the Sun).
Also, to reduce the darkness he created Kéenguenkom (the Moon), which became a powerful being. Xáleshen got in love with the moon and for that love was born Karro (the evening star), whom his mother gave him the domain of the tides.
When Kóoch separated the waters of the land, came the two oceans. One towards the rising and another to the west (the Atlantic and the Pacific). In the Atlantic created an island inhabited by giants, one of whom was Nosjthej who would be the father of El-lal, a strong, wise and beneficent hero whom tehuelches assigned the creation of their people.
Lighting the three elements of space, wind, light and clouds, Kóoch did emerge from the middle of the sea a huge island, which created life: birds, animals, insects and fish. To admire the work of Kóoch, the Sun sent light and heat, the Clouds bring the rain and Xoshem was responsible for creating pasture by moving the seeds on the ground.
Life was developing in a quiet way on the island of tehuelches, until the appearance of giants. From that island, El-lal moved to Patagonia all the animals that were his faithful friends. Therefore, El-lal is the central character of the mythical tehuelche, a hero, a master of the hunt and protector of them.
It was a swan who brought El-lal, depositing a child on the summit of Cerro Chalten (Fitz Roy today). For three days and three nights, protected by birds, the infant watched the new land. El-lal was the creator of the Chonek (tehuelches) revealed to men the secret of fire, bow and arrows, teach them to hunt and inculcated moral principles.
El-lal descended from the mountain and joined the people. He asked them to not surrendering him honors after his departure and he returned to his island carried by a majestic swan. In this mysterious island, El-lal awaits the Chonek, those who come resurrected are guided by Wendéunk, a spirit that takes account of the actions of each tehuelche.
